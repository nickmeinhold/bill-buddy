Open Banking Access & the ADR Path

A briefing on how to legally access Australian bank data under the CDR, what it costs to become accredited, and the faster routes that skip the accreditation entirely.

Research briefing · Shared for a group exploring open-banking access · July 2026

TL;DR

  • "Open banking" in Australia = the Consumer Data Right (CDR), regulated by the ACCC. To pull a person's bank data with their consent, you must either be an Accredited Data Recipient (ADR) or ride on top of someone who already is.
  • Becoming a full ADR is expensive and slow: roughly $30k–$300k upfront and 6–12+ months, plus unavoidable annual costs (insurance, AFCA membership, security assessment) whether you build it yourself or outsource.
  • There are two much cheaper on-ramps: the CDR Representative model (2–4 months, ride under a principal's accreditation) and using an intermediary like Basiq (weeks, ~$0 upfront, pay per user). Most teams should start on an intermediary and only pursue full ADR once volume justifies it.
  • The data flow is a standardised OAuth/consent handshake (see the diagram below): the user logs into their bank, grants consent, and data flows back to your backend over a secured connection. There is a legal duty to delete the data within 7 days of consent withdrawal.
  • The market gap is real: no Australian app offers the full Rocket Money feature set (bill negotiation + automated subscription cancellation). Existing players (Frollo, WeMoney, PocketSmith) stop at budgeting.

1What "open banking" actually is

Australia does not have a bank-run "open banking" API the way the UK does. Instead it has the Consumer Data Right (CDR), a government framework (regulated by the ACCC, with data standards set by the Data Standards Body) that gives consumers the right to share their own data with accredited third parties.

Banking was the first sector brought under the CDR, so in practice "open banking in Australia" means "the banking rules of the CDR." The key roles:

Data Holder

The bank. Legally required to expose the consumer's data through standardised CDR APIs when the consumer consents.

ADR

Accredited Data Recipient. The accredited party allowed to receive and use that data. Accreditation is granted by the ACCC.

CDR Representative

A party that operates under a principal ADR's accreditation, a lighter-weight way to participate without full accreditation.

Intermediary

A CDR-accredited company (e.g. Basiq) that does the hard part for you and hands you clean data via an ordinary API.

Our read

Start on an intermediary. It offloads the ACCC accreditation, the security assessment and the compliance burden onto Basiq, and gets you real bank data in weeks for roughly nothing upfront. The trade is a per-user margin tax and a dependency on their pricing and uptime. You only graduate to a full ADR once your per-user intermediary fees start to exceed the ~$80k–$130k/yr of running your own accreditation, which is a volume problem you would be lucky to have.

Why becoming a full ADR is a genuine undertaking

Full accreditation means the ACCC holds you responsible for the security and handling of consumer financial data. That entails an independent security assessment against the CDR information-security controls, ongoing audit and reporting obligations, dynamic client registration with every bank you want to connect to, and the engineering to speak the CDR's secured API profile correctly. It is doable by a technical team (hence the cheaper "DIY technical" row) but it is a multi-month project with real regulatory teeth, not a weekend integration.

4How the data flow works

Whichever route you take, the consumer-facing handshake is the same standardised consent flow. The user is redirected to their own bank to authenticate and approve, an authorisation code comes back, and your backend exchanges it for tokens over a secured (mutual-TLS) connection. Data then flows from the bank's CDR APIs. Note the deletion duty at the end.

5The market opportunity

Why this is worth a group's time. Australia looks ripe for a Rocket Money style product:

  • Australian households spend ~$50/month on streaming alone, up 16% year on year.
  • 46% of users actively rotate between services to manage costs.
  • 39% plan to cancel at least one subscription in the next 12 months.
  • 3 in 10 Australians lose up to $600/year on duplicate or unused services.
The gap

No Australian competitor offers Rocket Money's full feature set. Frollo, WeMoney and PocketSmith focus on budgeting. None offer bill negotiation or automated subscription cancellation.

6One application: a Rocket Money for Australia

For context, here is the specific product we have been building toward (a personal-finance app, "Bill Buddy"). This is one way to monetise CDR access; a group might pursue the same or something entirely different. Included so you can see how the access maps to a business.

Business model options we considered

Option A — Subscription + success fee (the Rocket Money model)

Free tier (see subscriptions, get alerts); premium at $8/month (cancellation concierge, bill monitoring); negotiation at 40% of first-year savings.

Option B — Pure affiliate

Free app, revenue entirely from switches: energy switch $50–$200, internet switch $50–$100, insurance referral $30–$150.

Option C — Hybrid (our current preference)

Free tier (subscription tracking, bill comparison); premium at $6/month (cancellation service, priority support); negotiation at 35% of savings or a flat $25 per successful negotiation; switches via affiliate commission without charging the user.

8Open questions for a group considering ADR

If the group wants to go further than "use an intermediary," these are the questions worth answering together before spending money:

  • Do we even need to be an ADR? For most product ideas, an intermediary covers you indefinitely. The full ADR case is usually about margin at scale or owning the data relationship, not about capability.
  • Representative vs full accreditation? The CDR Representative model (ride under a principal ADR) is the middle path: faster and cheaper than full accreditation, more control than an intermediary. Worth pricing against both.
  • Who holds the security and insurance liability? Full accreditation puts the compliance, insurance and AFCA obligations on the group. That is a real ongoing cost and a real risk to structure deliberately.
  • What is the actual product? Access is a means. The market gap (bill negotiation + subscription cancellation) is the most defensible wedge we have found, but the group should pick a wedge before optimising the access route.
  • Proof of concept first. The cheapest possible next step is a Basiq sandbox integration that pulls one real account's transactions. It de-risks everything downstream and costs almost nothing.
